---
title: 'Is AI Creating Cheap Design?'
description: 'The problem is not AI-generated design. The problem is what we choose to do with it.'
pubDate: 'Jun 12 2026'
---

A few days ago, I interviewed a designer.

During the conversation, he shared something interesting.

His previous company discouraged the use of AI-generated visuals because management believed AI produced "cheap design."

I have heard similar opinions before.

And honestly, I understand where they come from.

If you've spent enough time browsing AI-generated work online, you've probably seen the patterns.

The same lighting.

The same compositions.

The same visual styles.

The same polished but strangely generic results.

After a while, everything starts to look familiar.

And when everything looks familiar, it starts to feel cheap.

But I don't think AI is the reason.

The real question is:

What are we actually using AI for?

---

Many people treat AI as a replacement for thinking.

They write a prompt.

Generate a result.

Pick the version they like.

And move on.

In that workflow, AI becomes the designer.

The human simply becomes the person choosing from a list of options.

When that happens, the output often feels generic because the thinking behind it is generic.

The result is not cheap because AI created it.

It feels cheap because very little judgment was applied to it.

---

I use AI almost every day.

My team uses it too.

We generate concepts.

Explore directions.

Create visual references.

Test different layouts.

Challenge assumptions.

Sometimes we generate dozens of variations before deciding which direction deserves further attention.

In many ways, AI has become one of the fastest validation tools we have ever had.

Ideas that previously required hours can now be explored in minutes.

The value is not the image itself.

The value is the speed of exploration.

---

Design has never been about producing the first solution.

Good design comes from refining the solution.

Questioning it.

Improving it.

And then improving it again.

The best designers I have worked with are rarely satisfied with their first idea.

They constantly challenge their own work.

They look for flaws.

They ask uncomfortable questions.

They search for a better answer even after finding a good one.

AI simply allows that process to happen faster.

---

The irony is that AI has not made design less important.

It has made judgment more important.

When anyone can generate ten ideas within seconds, the competitive advantage is no longer creation.

The competitive advantage becomes taste.

Understanding.

Decision-making.

Knowing what to keep.

Knowing what to discard.

And understanding why.

---

Perhaps AI-generated work looks cheap when we stop thinking.

But when AI becomes part of a thoughtful design process, it becomes something else entirely.

Not a replacement for design.

A tool that allows designers to spend less time executing and more time thinking.

And that may be where the real value has always been.

---

*AI can generate solutions. Designers still decide which ones deserve to exist.*
